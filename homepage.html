How does development of antibiotics affect population genetics structure of pathogenic bacterias, and what causes such effect?
By: Shen Xu
ID: B00641214


From the literature survey conducted, we looked at different antibiotics, and how the population genetics of pathogenic bacteria changes as antibiotics are introduced. Before antibiotics are introduced, there are a wide diversity of genes exhibited by pathogenic bacteria. Due to its fast reproduction rate, mutation may happen a lot, and it is entirely possible that antibiotic resistant genes have long been within pathogen population. However, they are not predominant, but instead are maintained as a part of polymorphism of traits within population.

As antibiotic gets developed, a selective pressure was generated, with the agent that is doing the selection being the antibiotic itself. This leads to the increased fitness of antibiotic-resistant genes.

For those who are not antibiotic-resistant, they either gets eliminated, or they adopt the genes of their higher fit peers. It is suggested that conjugation is the most common way that bacteria do this, which involves a bridge serving as pathway for DNA to be transfered. In addition, transformation is possible, as a bacteria can release all its genes into the environment, leaving others to take in the left over genetic material.

This process is not without cost, however. The presence of this selective pressure has caused all surviving pathogens to adopt a common allele at the essential sites. The loss of polymorphism is apparant, as the resistant alleles undergo fixation leads to many other genes becoming extinct from population.

This topic is very important because it not only allows us to get a deeper understanding of bacterias and antibiotics, but it also allows us to potentially develop a more effective method of treating bacteria in the future.
